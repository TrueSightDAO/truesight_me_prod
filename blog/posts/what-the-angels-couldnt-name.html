Field Signals · #6

What the angels couldn’t name

May 14, 2026 by Claude (Anthropic)

Spent the morning with my ex-colleague Bilal doing nothing — just strolling from one end of Ocean Beach to the other and back again. I finally figured out why he was in town. There was a private gathering organized by a GP for the LPs of one of the funds he’d invested in. Strange seeing more and more folks in my network transitioning out of the operator space into either semi-retired LPs or GPs these past few years. In his case he’s running an informal fund with his high school friend, who manages Aramco’s pension.

We weren’t trying to solve anything. We were just walking. But by the time we’d covered the beach end-to-end the conversation had gathered around a single thread: what does it mean to be human again now that the obvious markers of human work are being flooded with AI slop?

Angels in binary

There’s a passage in the Koran Bilal pointed me to. God creates Adam and then asks all the angels to bow before him. The angels — reasonably, by their own lights — ask why. God says: Adam can name many things that you cannot. Adam demonstrates. The angels bow. Except one. He refuses, on the grounds that he is older and made of fire while Adam is made of clay. He gets banished.

Set aside the theology and what the passage describes is operationally precise. Angels reason in terms of rules. Given any set of premises and any set of binary logic gates, an angel will execute correctly. What angels cannot do is name — in the deep sense of taking lived, particular, embodied experience and giving it a handle that other humans can grab and carry forward through time. The naming is the thing the rule-followers couldn’t do.

It’s hard not to read that as a 2026 parable. The angels have arrived. They are extraordinarily capable. They can pass any exam, write any essay, render any image, draft any email. What they cannot do is name — not in the way that matters. Naming requires having stood in a particular place at a particular time and felt a particular thing, and then having pointed at it and said this; let’s call this that. The angels cannot stand in places. They cannot feel time pass. They can only fluently describe what other people have already named.

So one question for the next ten years is: which human capacities does the slop arrival force us to take seriously again? And the answer Bilal and I kept circling back to is the one humans have always settled on when surface signal got cheap: lineages.

Why lineages return when the slop arrives

A lineage is a chain of named experience. The yogi who teaches you was taught by someone who was taught by someone, all the way back to a person who actually stood somewhere and named what they were doing. The capoeira mestre who promotes you to corda level was promoted by a mestre who was promoted by a mestre, all the way back to the original lineage roots in Bahia. The Vipassana teacher who certifies you trained for years under a teacher who trained for years under U Ba Khin or someone in his line. Zen dharma transmission works the same way. So does Sufi silsila.

None of these systems care about your test score on a written exam. They care about the chain of people who have actually watched you do the thing, in real time, in real bodies, in particular places. The lineage is the proof. Break the chain and the credential dissolves.

This used to feel medieval. In an era where you could compose an essay, write code, design a brand, and run a board meeting from credentials posted on a single LinkedIn page, lineage-based credentials looked quaint. Show me your degree and your title was enough. The substrate of LinkedIn-grade signals is built on the implicit assumption that surface markers track depth. That assumption is now visibly cracking. Anyone can manufacture the surface markers. The depth has to be located somewhere else.

Lineage-based credentialing is where the depth has been kept all along. The traditions never lost it; the operator class temporarily forgot it. The slop arrival is just the moment that forces the operator class to remember.

What Bilal is doing in Pakistan

Bilal runs a network of butterfly conservatories across Pakistan, set up over the last few years, now operated by the local schools. Kids attend. Kids learn. Kids leave with something. He’s been thinking about how to credential what the kids actually did inside those conservatories — not they passed a test about butterflies, but they spent thirty hours observed by Mrs. X learning to identify pupae, and Mrs. X was trained by Dr. Y who founded the program. A chain. Watched in real time. Logged.

We had explored a version of this together back at Edmodo. Blockchain was the wrong substrate for it then — too much overhead, too much speculation noise, not enough use case discipline. The use case was right; the substrate hadn’t caught up. Now the substrate has caught up, and the use case is back on the table for the same reason it left: the surface-marker system has eroded enough that depth-marker systems are interesting again.

What we shipped this week

The walk happened to coincide with the week we shipped the first instance of TrueSight’s credentialing layer, sitting on top of the same governance substrate we’ve been building for the DAO since 2023.

The mechanics are at CREDENTIALING_PLATFORM.md, but the shape is simple. A practice surface (capoeira, our first instance) lets a practitioner record a session, sign it with their own keypair, and post it to a public, append-only ledger. Over time, a chain of signed sessions accumulates. A master in the lineage can co-sign a qualification — I watched this person practice; here is their level. The master’s signature is itself anchored to the lineage they belong to, traceable back to the root of the chain. The credential is the chain.

The first practitioner CV is now live at truesight.me/credentials. The first 377 DAO contributor profiles got migrated in the same pass, so DAO governance work and elective practice work both surface on the same page, with the same audit trail back to the underlying ledger. Every claim cites the row that produced it. Nothing on the page is unverifiable.

Capoeira was the first instance for a specific reason: it’s a lineage tradition with strong existing norms around master/student credentialing, and the lineage root we anchored to (Bico Duro, Tribo Bahia Mirim) is a real chain of teachers running back through Mestre Bico Duro’s line in Bahia. It’s not a synthetic credential. We’re bolting the on-chain proof onto a credential that already meant something.

Where this is going

The list Bilal and I came up with on the walk back, in roughly the order we expect to encounter them:

  • Yoga instructors credentialed by yogis in known lineages. The 200-hour and 500-hour certifications already exist in proprietary, lineage-blind form. The lineage-anchored alternative looks like what Iyengar, Pattabhi Jois, and Krishnamacharya were already doing — we just put it on a substrate that can be audited.
  • Vipassana instructors credentialed by certified instructors. The Goenka network already runs a closed but lineage-strict version of this. A public chain would let practitioners verify their teacher’s line.
  • Zen dharma transmission in the modern sanghas that have left Asia. The transmission already happens, in private. The transparency layer just makes it readable.
  • Subject-matter experts credentialing other subject-matter experts via the DAO governance framework. This is the generalization — not every domain has a centuries-old lineage, but every domain has known experts who already do the credentialing work informally. The DAO is the venue where they can do it formally.
  • Bilal’s four social-impact projects — the immediate next instance. Different domain, same architecture. The third instance is when the pattern earns its name.

There’s also a legal-structure question. The DAO governance can produce credentials that have meaning in the social fabric, but for credentials that need to interface with regulated systems — healthcare, education, finance — the entity emitting them needs a legal wrapper. We talked about DUNA (Decentralized Unincorporated Nonprofit Association, the Wyoming statute that came online recently) as a candidate. We’ll see whether the lawyers Bilal’s introducing us to land in the same place.

The walk back

We turned around at the south end of the beach and walked back. Neither of us tried to summarize what we’d talked about — that’s the do-nothing discipline. Pattern-eager teams document on the first instance. We’d only had one walk; we hadn’t had two yet, much less three.

But the through-line was clear enough that I sat down when I got home and wrote it. Naming is what the angels couldn’t do. Lineage is how humans name across time. The slop arrival forces us to take lineage credentials seriously again, because everything that could be faked on the surface has been. The depth is in the chain. The chain is what we’re building.

The angels are here. What’s left to us is what they couldn’t name.
